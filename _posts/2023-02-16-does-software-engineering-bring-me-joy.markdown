Yes. Such a simple answer, yet it took me close to 10 years to figure this out because, until recently, I didn't understand the difference between coding and programming.

I knew I liked software engineering. Otherwise, I wouldn't have relentlessly pursued it through years of self-studying, college classes at Caltech, MIT, and Stanford, classes at the local adult schools, online courses (MOOCs), and a computer science certificate from UCSC (University of California, Santa Cruz). But despite all of that, I mostly knew I was drawn to the field because I kept wanting to hang out with the software engineers at my company to learn the skills needed to do what they did while I was on the data analytics team.

Although I knew software engineering intrigued me, I kept asking myself if it was just that to me - a casual interest. I struggled because I would catch glimpses of excitement in me when I was coding, but there were frequent enough times of not knowing why I was spending the time to code what I was coding, which made me question how much I cared for this field.

But, because of a recent project I've been working on, it's finally dawned on me that my initial hesitance all comes down to the difference between coding and programming -- or at least what I understand the terms to mean. Coding is the lines of code written to solve computer science problems (think: LeetCode). It's what you need as a prerequisite to learn how to program. Programming, on the hand, is the act of coding to make programs to solve real problems.

Throughout the past 6 months, I've been passionately programming an Android mobile app called Neuroplasticity. The app makes a cognitive memory exercise given to me by a neurologist accessible to patients anywhere and anytime. Because the use case means a lot to me, it was easy to get into states of flow and excited about the code I was writing, knowing who this piece of software will greatly benefit. It still took an immense level of discipline and there definitely were frustrations with unexpected bugs that would just show up as soon as I thought I was done with a feature, but I genuinely enjoyed the entire process programming my app.

Coding LeetCode problems over and over again however...much harder to keep the elation going no matter how fascinated I was by the complexity of the problem, which I've been told is a common experience (unless you're a theoretical computer scientist perhaps!). The lack of a clear use or application for the code makes it difficult for me to sustain the motivation to sit at my desk for long periods of time to code. Undeniably, there is great value to coding practice exercises and I undergo giant sparks of joy when I finally figure out how to do a problem correctly or more efficiently, but it's not the main driving force for me to perfect my craft.

Throughout the years, I've spent more time than I would like to admit doing coding exercises rather than programming projects because all the classes I took heavily leaned towards teaching me how to code. The classes were centered around learning syntax, algorithms, and data structures, which are undoubtedly important. I was immersed and told to focus on the nuances and quirks of each language or framework including Java, C++, C, Python, JavaScript, HTML/CSS, SQL among many others. If I had known to take what I learned in class and make projects I cared about instead of stopping after completing the assigned coding exercises, that would've helped me realize sooner the extent of joy and delight I can get when writing code.

I'm happy to conclude after years of pondering that software engineering brings me joy. Solving real world problems through programs I make with code brings me immense joy. And I can't wait to make more projects that will continually improve in quality with time and in which I'm proud for others, including myself, to use to make life a little easier and/or more delightful.
